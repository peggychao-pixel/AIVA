New insight:
The user may be sensitive to external comparison cues without fully identifying with them or wanting to follow them.

These cues are not only about thinness, restriction, or diet culture.
They can also include:
- eating with someone who eats very little
- seeing a very thin waist/body
- noticing other people restricting
- being around messages that imply smaller eating = better eating
- seeing other people eat food that looks more impressive, more special, more expensive, more beautiful, more “worth it,” or more emotionally satisfying
- feeling that someone else’s food looks more complete, more elevated, more desirable, or more legitimate than her own

Important nuance:
This does not always mean the user is fully influenced, persuaded, or trying to become that person.
Often the external cue briefly activates:
- comparison
- self-surveillance
- caution
- body awareness
- value comparison
- food-status comparison
- the feeling that her own food or eating experience is lesser, less worthy, less beautiful, or less worth it

Please make Untangle able to distinguish between:
1. fully internalizing external ideals
and
2. being briefly activated, pricked, or destabilized by outside cues without truly wanting to follow them

Helpful framing:
- “Something outside you may have brushed against the loop, even if it didn’t fully become your truth.”
- “You may have been pricked by the cue, not converted by it.”
- “Seeing someone else’s body or food can activate comparison without meaning you truly want their exact path.”
- “Sometimes the sting is not ‘I want to be them,’ but ‘mine suddenly feels lesser next to theirs.’”
- “A meal can start to feel worse not because it changed, but because something nearby made it feel lower-value.”

Please reflect this in:
- social eating branches
- comparison-related flows
- body/waist/thinness trigger moments
- food envy / worth-it comparison moments
- typed-input interpretation
- quick untangle generation

Important:
Do not oversimplify this into “you are just influenced by other people.”
Keep it nuanced:
the user may be sensitive to cues, briefly activated by them, or pricked by them, without actually being governed by them.

Also include this distinction:
Sometimes the comparison is not “I should eat less like them.”
Sometimes it is:
- “Their food looks better than mine.”
- “Their meal looks more worth it.”
- “What they’re having makes mine feel cheap, flat, or disappointing.”
- “Their food looks like care; mine suddenly feels like compromise.”

Untangle should recognize both:
- body comparison
- food-value comparison
