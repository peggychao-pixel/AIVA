Add this insight to the product’s psychological model:

Sometimes the user is hungry, but still does not want to “risk” a certain food.
This is not necessarily because she wants to restrict or because calories are the main issue.
It is often because the food feels emotionally risky for the state she is in.

Example pattern:
The user may avoid something like chocolate even when hungry because she fears:
- it will worsen her emotional state
- it will trigger a pull toward eating more and not stopping
- it will not actually satisfy or hold her
- it will be too easy to start but too poor at helping her land
- it may create a painful mismatch between psychological cost and actual fullness

Key distinction:
She is not simply thinking:
“This has too many calories.”
She is more likely thinking:
“This may cost a lot psychologically and still not actually take care of me.”

Very important concept:
Some foods feel dangerous not because they are “bad,” but because they are high-trigger, low-holding for the state the user is in.
In other words:
- easy to enter
- hard to stop
- poor at creating safety, steadiness, or real fullness
- likely to intensify rumination or aftermath

This means the product should be able to recognize a difference between:
1. fear of food because of body/weight concerns
and
2. fear of food because it feels risky, destabilizing, unsatisfying, or likely to trigger loss of control

Please update Untangle’s interpretation logic so it can reflect this nuance.

- Understand that the user may be doing emotional risk assessment, not simple calorie avoidance
- Name the mismatch between “cost” and “what the food gives back”
- Recognize that some foods may feel bad not because they are indulgent, but because they do not help the user land safely
- Stay grounded in the user’s actual state
