Please update Untangle in two connected ways:
1. refine the psychological model
2. explore a UI direction that feels more like “untangling” and less like filling out a form

Important:
Do NOT change the core product category.
Untangle should remain an emotional-support / rumination-interruption product for inner conflict around food, craving, anticipation, guilt, overwhelm, and aftermath.
Do NOT turn it into a calorie tracker, nutrition coach, meal planner, generic ED app, or life advice bot.

-----------------------------------
PART 1 — PSYCHOLOGICAL MODEL UPDATES
-----------------------------------

A. First-tap should not narrow too early
Current issue:
Some “before eating” branches become too restrictive too fast. A first-layer tap can make the experience feel like the user has already been boxed into one explanation.

Design principle:
First tap = signal, not conclusion.
Each branch = lens, not verdict.

Please revise the branching logic so:
- first-layer options identify what is active, not what the whole problem is
- second-layer prompts expand understanding rather than overcommitting too quickly
- reflections sound like grounded hypotheses, not therapist-style conclusions
- mixed emotional reality is preserved longer

Use softer framing such as:
- “Part of what may be making this hard...”
- “It sounds like...”
- “This may be less about X itself, and more about...”

Avoid deterministic lines like:
- “You’re not really struggling with X, you’re actually struggling with Y”

B. Capture emotional risk, not just calories or restriction
Untangle should understand that sometimes the user is hungry but still does not want to risk a certain food.
This is not necessarily because she wants to restrict or because calories are the core problem.

Often the real question is:
- Will this food actually hold me?
- Will it make my emotional state worse?
- Will it trigger more pull and less control?
- Will it cost a lot psychologically and still not satisfy anything real?

Important distinction:
Some foods feel dangerous not because they are “bad,” but because they are high-trigger and low-holding for the state the user is in:
- easy to enter
- hard to stop
- poor at creating real steadiness or safety
- likely to intensify rumination or aftermath

Please make sure Untangle can distinguish between:
1. fear of food because of body/weight concerns
and
2. fear of food because it feels destabilizing, risky, unsatisfying, or hard to land safely

Treat this as a core internal question:
“Will this actually hold me?”

C. Add this new insight: food-shape / whole-ness anxiety
Please add another important psychological insight:

Sometimes what distresses the user is not only what she ate, but the fact that it does not feel like a coherent, recognizable whole.

For example:
A combination of foods may actually meet different needs and help her feel more physically or emotionally held.
But if it does not look like “a real snack” or “a proper meal” or “one complete thing,” she may feel agitated, suspicious, or dysregulated afterward.

This is not just about nutrition or calories.
It is about lack of form coherence.

Key idea:
The user may feel distress when the food does not feel like:
- one recognizable unit
- one named thing
- one socially legible snack or meal
- one whole, coherent object she can mentally accept and “close”

This can create panic such as:
- “This doesn’t feel like a real snack”
- “This feels too random / pieced together”
- “This doesn’t feel like a whole”
- “This feels abnormal even if it helped”

Please reflect this nuance in Untangle’s interpretation logic.

Desired behavior:
- recognize that distress may come from lack of coherence, not just from the food itself
- understand that the mind may be looking for a form it can accept, not only a calorie count or emotional fix
- help the user feel less defective if the food was “assembled” rather than textbook
- distinguish between “not elegant” and “not valid”

Helpful language direction:
- “Part of what may feel unsettling is not just what you ate, but that it doesn’t feel like one whole, nameable thing.”
- “This may be less about whether it was wrong, and more about whether it felt coherent enough to mentally land.”
- “Sometimes the food can help, but still feel hard to trust if it doesn’t look like a complete unit.”

D. Make high-hunger branches more concrete
Current issue:
In branches like “I’m so hungry I feel less in control,” responses such as “Eat first” are emotionally valid but too abstract.

When the user is this hungry, she may not need more explanation.
She may need help moving from vague overwhelm to one concrete edible next step.

Please revise those branches so they:
- validate the hunger state
- reduce global overwhelm
- move from “eat something” to “find one concrete thing”
- help the user feel she does not have to solve everything at once

-----------------------------------
PART 2 — UI DIRECTION UPDATE
-----------------------------------

Current concern:
The current box-by-box option UI is clear, but it can feel too much like:
- a questionnaire
- a symptom checker
- a decision tree
- a form with emotional labels

This weakens the core feeling of Untangle.
The interface should feel less like answering boxes and more like gradually loosening a knot of thoughts, pulls, fears, and spirals.

Important:
Do NOT make the UI abstract, confusing, or purely decorative.
Do NOT sacrifice usability.
Keep interactions simple and tappable on mobile.

Please explore a hybrid interaction model where:
- the center of the screen represents a tangled mental knot / messy thought cluster / emotional spiral
- around it are selectable thoughts, fears, or “threads”
- each option feels like one strand of the knot, not a diagnostic category
- when tapped, a selected thread becomes highlighted / separated / gently pulled out
- the knot visually softens, loosens, or partially clears as the conversation progresses
- the experience feels like untangling, not form completion

Design principles:
1. Keep first interaction lightweight and clear
2. Preserve fast tapping and low cognitive load
3. Make emotional states feel visualized, not just labeled
4. Each choice should feel like a thread or pull, not a box in a form
5. The visual system should move from chaos -> clarity
6. Keep the tone grounded, emotionally intelligent, and not overly whimsical
7. Avoid making it feel like a quiz, mood tracker, horoscope app, or meditation toy

Please propose:
1. a refined UI concept description
2. how this can stay highly usable on mobile
3. what should remain simple cards/buttons versus what should become thread-based
4. a minimal implementation plan without rebuilding the whole app

Overall goal:
Make Untangle feel more like untangling.
Keep it emotionally precise, low-shame, usable, and distinct from generic chatbot forms.
