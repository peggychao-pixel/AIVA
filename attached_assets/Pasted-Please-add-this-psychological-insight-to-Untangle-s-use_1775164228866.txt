Please add this psychological insight to Untangle’s user model and interpretation logic.

Important:
Do not reduce this to portion control, discipline, or simple guilt about eating.
This is about legitimacy, qualification, and whether the user feels her way of eating “counts” enough to justify eating at all.

New insight:
Sometimes the user feels she has “no right to eat” not only because of hunger confusion or desire guilt, but because the eating does not feel legitimate enough in form.

Examples:
- eating only a very small amount
- eating across multiple stalls / places but only a little at each
- grazing instead of having one clear meal
- eating in a way that does not feel like a proper, complete, socially legible unit

In these moments, the user may feel:
- “This doesn’t count enough to justify eating”
- “If I only ate a little, I don’t have the right to eat this”
- “If I’m eating in bits and pieces, it feels less legitimate”
- “This feels like I’m taking without having enough basis”

Important nuance:
The distress is not only about amount.
It is about whether the eating feels legitimate, coherent, and justified enough.

This often overlaps with:
- permission / qualification loops
- food-shape / whole-ness anxiety
- shame around grazing or non-unit eating
- feeling that only a “real meal” earns the right to eat
- feeling that small bites or fragmented eating are less valid

Please make Untangle able to recognize the difference between:
1. “I ate too much”
and
2. “The way I ate doesn’t feel legitimate enough to let myself eat peacefully”

Helpful language direction:
- “Part of what may feel bad is not only how much you ate, but that the eating doesn’t feel legitimate enough in your mind.”
- “This may feel loaded because it happened in bits and pieces, not in a form that feels easy to accept.”
- “Sometimes the shame is less about quantity and more about feeling like the eating doesn’t ‘count’ in a way your mind can permit.”
- “It may be hard to settle when the eating doesn’t feel like a clear enough unit to mentally stand on.”

emotionally precise, low-shame, psychologically sharp, and focused on untangling rather than moralizing.
