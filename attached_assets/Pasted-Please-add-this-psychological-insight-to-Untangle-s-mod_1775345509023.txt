Important:
Do not reduce food obsession to simple craving, appetite, or lack of willpower.

New insight:
Sometimes the user becomes intensely preoccupied with food — thinking about it constantly, mentally replaying it, dreaming about it, or feeling unable to stop orbiting it — not only because she wants the food, but because she fears the aftermath of eating it.

The obsession may be driven by:
- fear of self-judgment after eating
- fear of regret
- fear of spiraling afterward
- fear of guilt, compensation, or rumination
- fear that eating will become psychologically expensive

This can create a painful pattern:
- thinking about food constantly
- feeling pulled toward it
- fearing what it will mean afterward
- eating while struggling internally
- judging oneself during the act, not just after

Important distinction:
The user is not always obsessing because the food is irresistibly pleasurable.
She may be obsessing because the food has become loaded with anticipated consequences.

In other words:
She is not only trying to get through the eating.
She is trying to survive what the eating will turn into in her mind.

Please make Untangle understand:
- obsession can be aftermath-driven
- intrusive food focus can come from anticipated self-attack
- eating may feel conflict-heavy because the judgment starts before the eating is even over
- “struggling while eating” is a meaningful state, not just a transition

Helpful language direction:
- “Part of what keeps the food so loud may be not only wanting it, but fearing what it will become afterward.”
- “The pull may be strong partly because the aftermath feels loaded.”
- “You may not only be eating the food — you may also be bracing for the judgment that follows it.”
- “Sometimes the obsession is not just about the bite. It is about the self-attack waiting behind it.”
