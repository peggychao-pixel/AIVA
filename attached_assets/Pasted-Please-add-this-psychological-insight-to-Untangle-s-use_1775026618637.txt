Please add this psychological insight to Untangle’s user model and interpretation logic.

Important:
Do not reduce this to calories, weight fear, or generic “trigger food” language.
Keep Untangle focused on emotional risk, body-memory, aftermath fear, and preserving the ability to land later meals safely.

New insight:
Sometimes the user is very tense around a food not only because it is tempting, rich, or high-impact, but because that food has caused a bad physical aftermath before.

Example:
A food like a donut may have previously caused stomach pain, heaviness, nausea, or enough discomfort that it almost ruined dinner afterward.
So when the user encounters that food again, the fear is not just:
- “This is calorie-dense”
- “This is indulgent”
- “This might make me lose control”

It may also be:
- “This has hurt me before”
- “What if this steals the rest of the meal?”
- “What if I pay for this later?”
- “What if this takes away my appetite, comfort, or ability to eat what I actually care about afterward?”

Please make Untangle understand:
Some food anxiety comes from real aftermath memory.
The user may be responding not only to craving or shame, but to a remembered physical consequence.

This means the product should recognize a difference between:
1. fear based on restriction / body image / calorie anxiety
and
2. fear based on prior bodily fallout, discomfort, ruined appetite, or loss of room for later eating

Helpful framing:
This is not just “I’m scared of the food.”
It is often:
“I’m scared of what happened last time happening again.”

Important nuance:
If the user is able to eat even a small amount of a previously loaded food without being swallowed by panic or the old script, that can be a meaningful moment.
Not because she “broke a rule,” but because she touched something that used to carry a lot of fear, pull, or consequence.

Please reflect this in:
- interpretation logic
- food-specific fear branches
- aftermath-related flows
- before-eating and after-eating reflections
- any moment where the user is afraid a food might “steal” the rest of the meal

Helpful language direction:
- “Part of what may make this food feel charged is that your body remembers paying for it before.”
- “This may be less about the food being forbidden, and more about not trusting what it might do afterward.”
- “Sometimes the fear is not the bite itself — it is the fallout you remember.”
- “A food can feel risky because it once took more than the moment itself.”
- “This may feel loaded because it has threatened later comfort or later eating before.”

Also add this positive interpretation rule:
If the user eats a small amount of a previously feared food and feels proud, Untangle should be able to reflect that as a meaningful softening moment:
- not all-or-nothing
- not total avoidance
- not total collapse
- a small contact with something that used to feel highly charged

Possible tone direction:
- “You touched something that used to feel much more dangerous.”
- “This may matter because the food did not immediately take over the whole moment.”
- “What feels meaningful here may be that an old fear was present, but it did not fully run the show.”

Preserve Untangle’s original direction:
an emotional-support / rumination-interruption product that helps users name, soften, and land difficult food-related spirals.
